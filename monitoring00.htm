Availability and Response Time Monitoring

 

As your application runs in the IBM Cloud, the Cloud is automatically monitoring for availability and URL response time.   These two metrics give a basic understanding on how your application is doing.  Along with those performance metrics the Cloud also keeps track of major events in an application’s execution such as binding to Services, URL definitions and outages which caused an automatic restart.

 

IBM also has advanced application monitoring that covers a vast amount of other metrics, a topic that will be covered later in this course.

 

The metrics IBM Cloud provides for an application out-of-the-box are:

 

Availability - the ability to determine if an application is up and healthy

 

Response Time - the ability to time application requests to determine overall speed; at least what a typical Customer might experience

 

Actions - things the cloud did for the application, both automatically or requested, to keep it running healthy

 

Let’s get an understanding of how to use these metrics in relation to keeping your application healthy

 

Step 0

 

Log into the IBM Cloud and display the Dashboard.  Click on your running application (or Toolchain) and you will arrive at the application detail display. 

 

 

On the left side of that display you will see the link for monitoring.  Click on that link and you should see the display below:

 

 

Click on the View All Tests button in yellow.

 

Step 1

 

An example to help you understand what you are seeing:

 

Your first test shown are the synthetic tests performed by globalized software testing agents.  IBM Cloud has several geographical locations around the world that are contacting URLs with a robotic HTTPS request, timing that request and looking at the returned response to determine if those applications are up.  What is shown in the display below is your deployed application that is showing available, globally, 100% of the time and has an average URL response time of .25 seconds. 

 

This average is from all the response times of all geographical locations added together then divided by the number of testing locations.  It is a sanity check on what a Customer might be experiencing with your application but it is not the actual response any particular Customer has been measured with.

 

For example, in London a response time from a testing agent might be showing .255 seconds, in Australia it might be showing .447 seconds but in Dallas, where your application is running, it could be a bit faster at .057 seconds.  The total response time from all locations is .957 seconds before dividing by the 3 locations which equals an average of .25 seconds.  This gives you a ballpark idea of what a random Customer might experience with your application.

 

 

In the IBM Cloud Monitoring facility you can get a history of how your application has performed since it started by just scrolling down a bit in your web browser.  These robotic testing agents have been sending requests to applications every 15 minutes or so and the results for your application tests are graphed over time for you to see.

 

Step 2

 

In this example, in your display below, 3 testing agents were used, one in London another in Melbourne and finally in Dallas where the application is running.  If you look real close you can see that the farther away a testing agent is to where the application is running the longer the response takes.  In the example below, you can see Melbourne is consistently clocking up the longest response time starting at 9 PM until the next days’ 8 AM test where London takes over as being the slowest location.  Why you ask? Not sure yet but if that trend continues it would be something you should look into.

 

 

Notice as well, on the far left are red and yellow alert emoji’s.  These are indicating the preset thresholds (at 7.5 seconds for warning and 10.75 seconds for bad).  None of those thresholds are in jeopardy of being tripped at this time.  At the rate your application is being robotically tested there is an estimated 8,640 tests that will take place over a 30 day period and should be factored as part of your application’s overall usage.   Even though it’s a trivial test it does appear like 5 more Customers are using your product over the course of a day.

 

Step 3

 

If you wanted to add additional testing agents, you would scroll the display to the extreme far right until you see the display below.  You would click on the Add more locations.

 

 

By default, IBM Cloud automatically sets up a few testing locations around the world for this purpose but one can add even more testing locations should another location be relevant to your application.  Just click on the location below and it will turn blue and will begin participating in monitoring your application.

 

Step 4

 

 

 

IBM Monitoring also keeps track of all the events that have occurred with your application.  These help to remind you of what has taken place during the robotic testing period and in some cases can explain any abnormalities that may have been detected.

 

 

Most of the items you will see above in the display are from the Toolchain Build and Deploy Tools making entries as it stops, restages, adds Services and updates URLs.  If the IBM Cloud detected a crash and automatically restarted your application, that too would be shown as an entry.  It’s a good idea to look at this list from time-to-time just to see if your application is crashing for some reason.

 

That is pretty much the out-of-the-box monitoring you get with your application and in some cases you may want more detail.  IBM Cloud has additional monitoring for this purpose which will be addressed next.
