Implementing Continuous Delivery for your Cloud developed application

 

Practicing the Continuous Delivery Methodology

 

In some respects what has been accomplished by the two Toolchain’s themselves is the basis of the Continuous Delivery methodology.  You have two executables running in the IBM Cloud; each running out of different repositories containing your sample application.  One Toolchain is used for Development and one for Production with a safe physical barrier separating them; a barrier that will only be crossed when the application is tested and deemed perfect and its features are ready to be made public. 

 

Now it is time to demonstrate how to take full advantage of the Toolchains for the purpose of Production, Development and the Testing of your new features; a methodology that enforces a non-disruptive deployment for your Customers. 

 

Below is what all your hard work in your college courses have accomplished

 

 

Reference the diagram above as we walk through our scenario

 

A question you may ask?

 

Why two repositories?  With all that learning about repository branches and how they keep versions separated, why not just use one repository with two branches (master and dev)?  The answer is you can.  However, it is too easy to accidently push something (on purpose or by accident) to the wrong branch, and in the case of the master branch, you would have just corrupted your production version in one press of the Enter Key.   It is far harder to accidently push into a separate repository for they are already protected against that type of behavior.

 

Having them separate, as they are above, as long as you just develop in the Development Repository – Production is kept absolutely safe.  But don’t despair, all that git stuff you learned will reappear when we learn some advanced topics around developing locally at the desktop using Continuous Delivery in the Cloud. 

 

Development scenario

 

Now, let us put the two Toolchain methodology just created into a real life situation to better understand its value.  Let us create a scenario in your course; a scenario comprised of a typical developer named Paul who will design, develop and test a new feature right from the Cloud.  Paul will use all these tools in conjunction with his sample application, and at the end, update the Production Toolchain to make the feature just created his latest public version.

 

Meet Paul, he is a 5 year experienced and intuitive software developer with some basic Node.js background and a hankering to acquire a skillset surrounding the Cloud.  He has been working in a typical retail corporation, in their Human Resources department, where he has just learned that a corporate golf tournament has been announced to raise money for the corporation’s charity fund.  What is needed is to get everyone in the company, who golfs or spectates; on board.  What is required is some sort of online sign-up sheet.

 

Paul decides it would look great if someone in Human Resources created this, even greater, it should be him due to his knowledge that employee promotions are coming around toward the end of the year. 

 

Paul is thinking, if he uses his sample web server application, provided to him by his curiosity around Quick2Cloud, he could quickly modify it and create a webpage saving away everyone’s name who signs up.  To top it off, Paul is eager to use Continuous Delivery to push out his changes until he has it all working just the way he wants.

 

How Paul develops in the Cloud

 

Paul already has his sample web server application running, did that last week, bound with an IBM Cloudant Service no less and just finished setting them up for Continuous Delivery by creating a couple of Toolchains.  The sample application is already running in Production and Development, both with URLs based off their Toolchain names.  What is left to do is create a simple golf signup sheet and write the employee’s name to his Cloudant database.

 

Let us look at the tools available to Paul provided by the IBM Toolchain

 

Step 0

Paul Logs into the IBM Cloud at www.ibm.com/cloud.  Then he clicks on the  drop-down menu on the left side of the display.  Paul remembers from his Quick2Cloud College studying to click on DevOps selection. 

 

In the list of Toolchains that now appears, Paul clicks on the one he previously created for Development which brings him to this list below of supplied online Continuous Delivery tools.  Excitingly, Paul wants to really encompass the Cloud, he begins with adding an entry in the Issues tool about what he thinks he wants to do.

 

He clicks on the Issues Tile.

 

 

Step 1

 

Paul likens this tool to a fancy diary, enters in thoughts of what he wants to accomplish with the sample web server application.  He has some experience with Node.js so he thinks if he reads the existing sample code he might be able to figure out how to add a web page as his first feature then how to store the employee names in the Cloudant database as his second.

 

As a benefit, if Paul had someone else developing with him they too could use the Issues Tool and get an understanding, or even comment about what Paul wants.

 

Paul fills in the fields of the issue tool then submits writings

 

 

Step 2

 

Paul now clicks on the Toolchain Eclipse Editor tile for the first time, that’s the tile found on the Development Toolchain. Eclipse will take a moment to load and as part of that load will clone the code from the Toolchain repository and into its Eclipse Workspace Repository.  Upon presenting itself for the first time, Paul’s files will not be displayed until he un-hides them by clicking on the yellow marker below.

 

Click on the yellow marker, the sample application files will appear. 

 

Click on the qvsample.js file which is the web server application

 

 

Step 3

 

In front of Paul (shown below) is the qvsample.js Node.js code with all its comments and information on its inner-workings.  After reading through this code Paul will come to realize all that needs to be done is to copy the existing home page (index.htm) and name it golf.htm.   Next, craft some words in golf.htm (using Microsoft Word) welcoming everyone to the charity tournament.  Finally, Paul will go into the redirect.htm page and replace his golf login page as the home page.  At that point, he wants to see if these modifications will work.

 

Once Paul makes the changes, and he does it all right in the Eclipse Editor, he will have two files that have been transformed into the git modified state.  His first file is golf.htm which will be considered a new file that he edited and the modified version of redirect.htm.  Before he commits them and makes it real, he wants to try these changes out. 

 

Eclipse, in the IBM Toolchain Tool, has a special capability that allows Paul to temporarily bypass the commit and push git protocols and run his application directly out of the Eclipse Workspace Directory.  To begin his test, Paul will press the yellow icon below that looks like a play button. 

 

Pressing this will trigger the Development Pipeline Delivery tool in the Toolchain and the current running application will be stopped then restarted with the application code in the Eclipse Workspace Directory.  The blue status bar to that icon’s left announces when the Development Toolchain has restarted the application and the golf homepage is ready to view.

 

Pressing the second icon highlighted in yellow below, Paul sees his new golf home page in a separate web browser tab.

 

Paul Clicks on that yellow icon

 

 

Step 4

 

It seems from looking at the homepage results in the browser, like the rest of us, Paul introduced a typo in the golf.htm page.  Quickly going back into the Eclipse Editor, he fixes the formatting error then runs it again by pressing the yellow icon that looks like a play button.  To see if his changes display correctly, Paul presses the second yellow button again and invokes the Brower to view his homepage.

 

Step 5

 

Paul’s Eclipse workspace testing is done and now it is time to commit his change to the Eclipse Workspace Repository and then into the Toolchain Repository where the Pipeline Delivery Tool will automatically be notified and deploy it.  What needs to be done now is to switch to the built-in Eclipse git facility, a facility outside the Eclipse Editing Workspace; a facility provided to Eclipse for use in the IBM Cloud.

 

On the left side of the Eclipse editor, just under the Root icon is the git icon highlighted in yellow.  This icon will switch you out of the Eclipse edit mode into  git repository mode where all the git terms like commit and push reside; but just for the Eclipse Workspace.  After Paul decided that this one feature is all he wants to commit at this time, he now clicks on this git icon and begins placing his first feature in the annals of git immortality.

 

Click on the yellow git icon

 

Step 6

 

Hang in there; stick by this course because you are almost done.  If you are feeling a little overwhelmed at the moment, don’t worry, Quick2Cloud will assist you when the time comes and when you are doing this with your own application. 

 

OK, Paul, it’s time to commit this change to the Eclipse Workspace Repository.  Displayed as horizontal blue bars, (see diagram below) are the files that have been modified since Paul started editing for his golf tournament.  As you can see, Paul created a new golf.htm file, modified the index.htm file (which was the old home page), modified the redirect.htm file (holds the redirect language) and in the process some configuration file got modified as well.

 

Tip: if you click to expand those horizontal lines, git will display what changed in those files so you can have one last look before you fire that commit off.

 

The commit message Paul wrote explains it all, this is his first feature of the golf tournament.

 

Paul now clicks on the Commit button

 

 

Step 7

 

Yes!  Feature one has been committed to the Eclipse Workspace Repository.  Because it is a repository under git methodology Paul could actually back it out by restoring to the commit prior, which in his case, would be the original codebase.  

 

In the grand scheme of things Paul can now start his second feature (storing names in the database) or he can push this committed feature up to the Toolchain Repository where the Pipeline Delivery Tool can take hold of it.  For the purpose of this course, let us push it into the Toolchain Repository so we can explore more of the Toolchain mechanics involved in deploying.

 

Paul is excited to push his first feature into the Toolchain Repository.  By clicking on push, he selects the push All option.  Still very eager for results, Paul wants to see his new feature officially running in the IBM Cloud under Continuous Delivery, making this moment memorable in his short but budding Cloud life.

 

It’s time for Paul to click on push

 

 

Step 8

 

The Continuous Delivery tool, Delivery Pipeline, sensed that Paul committed the feature to the IBM Repository.  If you look on the Build tile under Delivery Pipeline, you will see a checkbox toward the bottom that indicates to automatically restart the Build and Deploy stages on a Commit.  On that commit, the Toolchain Repository took it upon itself to start Paul’s application (with his first feature) and once it started; halted his original application, basically swapping one version for another without disrupting any Customers that might have been momentarily working with his web server. 

 

If Paul had Customers using his Development level golf program (but he doesn’t yet) at the time of the push they would not be disrupted because of the way that the Delivery Pipeline tool works.  One click before the push the Customer would be in the Quick2Cloud sample application, next click after the push, Customer would be on his golf application with a golf homepage.  

 

So Close!

 

What’s left is just a quick update to the Pipeline Deploy Tool Deploy Script and Continuous Delivery will be finally in place for this sample application.
