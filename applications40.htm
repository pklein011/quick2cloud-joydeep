IBM Cloud Dashboard

 

In working with the IBM Cloud, a full Dashboard exists containing information about your running application as well as other aspects of their Cloud.  This course will introduce you to that dashboard by starting with a narrow focus around your sample application and its resources.  Later, Quick2Cloud College will have you working with the dashboard in its other areas as well.  In each course, we will take the dashboard one step at a time.

 

Step 9

 

Log into the IBM Cloud at www.ibm.com/cloud and enter your IBM Cloud ibmID and password.  The first display you get should be the dashboard.  For now, just focus on the area of the dashboard that is enclosed in the red dotted lines below.

Note: if the Dashboard is not the first item when you login, click on the  on the top left and select dashboard from the drop-down list.

 

IBM Cloud Dashboard

 

 

 

In the image above, your application is hidden under the Cloud Foundry Apps.  If you had Services defined (Course 101) those Services would be listed under Services.  Click on the Cloud Foundry line, this causes the line to expand revealing your application in the Cloud’s Resource list

 

 

 

In the image above, clicking on the qvsample (name of your Quick2Cloud sample application) takes you to a more detailed resource view of the application as it interacts with the Cloud. 

 

Note: on the qvsample line, toward the far right, are three dots, if you click on them you can Stop, Restart or Delete the application from the Cloud. 

 

Click on the qvsample name.

 

 

In the image above, this is the detailed application resource display.  This is a place you will be spending a lot of time as you craft your own application and want to tinker with it.  Within this display are some very important bits of information that you will need.  Let’s go over each one.

 

In the detail display, it provides a quick launch (2) to your Application’s home page via the Visit App URL link.  When the supplied URL is rather large and hard to remember, this is a handy feature to have.  Just click here and a new window will open with your application’s home page rendered in it. 

 

IBM allows you to change the URL of your Application in Routes (3) as long as the name is unique across the IBM Cloud.  For now, let us change your URL to something easy to remember while in college.  Click on Routes (3), then click on Edit Routes, now you will see the dialog presented below.  Overtype the IBM generated name with your new name (in yellow) and click on Save.  From this point forward your URL for this application is ibmID-qvsample.mybluemix.net with you substituting your IBMid that you registered with.

 

 

 

Tip: For uniqueness, always start your URL with the first part of your IBM ibmID.  For example, mine would be pklein011-qvsample.mybluemix.net

 

Oh yes, eventually the IBM Cloud will charge a Runtime Cost (4) if you build-out a large enough application.  Later you will see how that fee is calculated but for now you are using IBM’s Lite Plan and that has no Runtime Charge associated with it.  

 

However, as mentioned before, you are restricted to an application and memory quota which limits you to just a couple of simultaneous running applications and in total about 512M of memory.  This will all change when you eventually upgrade to a less restrictive plan.  Quick2Cloud recommends that you learn about the Cloud on the Lite Plan then you will have a better idea of what plan you will ultimately want to invest in.

 

Logs

 

One of the most important uses of this display is to view your application’s log (1) and see any messages or errors you introduced into the sample application, specifically the change you made logging out the operating systems language.  Click on Logs.  Here you will be directed to a log from your running application and it will contain not only your application messages but also the messages from the Cloud in deploying your application.  It’s the place to look at first if you suspect you have a problem with a URL that won’t respond. 

 

Note:  This is where you will find all messages that were created with Node.js console.log() function.

 

 

In the above image, showing is the format of the log display for your application.  Notice, by default, it not only logs what your application writes out to console.log() but other information about the application such as the HTTP Header information on every request.  Filter the log by clicking on either All or Errors.

 

Very good! You are now done with Course 100 and should have a grasp on how to get a locally developed application from your desktop up and into the Cloud.  You should also know where to look in IBM’s Dashboard to see the application and how to get hold of its logs for debugging.   From this point on, you can modify your application and issue your script again to get your latest version in the Cloud.
